How Templar Token Emissions Actually Work

If you're not deep in the Bittensor ecosystem, this section explains the mechanics that drive dilution.

The Big Picture

Bittensor is a network of 128 "subnets" — think of them as competing AI projects. Each day, the network mints 3,600 new TAO tokens (~$1M/day) and distributes them to subnets based on how much stake (investment) they attract. Templar (Subnet 3) gets the biggest share: 6.86%, or ~247 TAO/day (~$71K/day).

This is how miners get paid for providing GPU compute. There's no revenue, no customers — it's all token inflation. The cost of everything (GPU compute, model training, coordination) is funded by minting new tokens.

Two Tokens, Two Layers of Dilution

Each subnet has its own token (called an "alpha" token) that trades against TAO in a liquidity pool. For Templar, the alpha token is what you buy on CoinGecko. Here's how the two layers work:

Layer 1: TAO Emissions (network level)

Every day, 3,600 new TAO are minted across the whole Bittensor network. TAO has a 21M max supply, ~8.9M currently circulating. Annual inflation: ~13%. This was 26%+ before the Dec 2025 halving cut daily emissions from 7,200 to 3,600.

Layer 2: Alpha Emissions (subnet level)

Each subnet also emits its own alpha tokens. Templar's alpha has a 21M max supply, ~4.22M circulating. New alpha is created each block and split between: miners (GPU providers), validators (quality checkers), and the subnet creator. This is the direct source of the supply growth you see in the charts above.

What Does TAO Flowing Into the SN3 Pool Actually Do?

This is confusing because it cuts both ways. Here's the mechanic:

Templar's alpha token trades in an AMM liquidity pool (like Uniswap) against TAO. The pool has two sides: TAO and alpha tokens. When ~247 TAO/day flows in from emissions:

Bullish effect: More TAO backing each alpha token

More TAO in the pool means the pool's "reserves" grow on the TAO side. Since alpha price = TAO in pool / alpha in pool, adding TAO to the pool pushes the alpha price up. It's like the subnet getting a daily cash injection. This is why Templar's market cap has been able to grow despite constant selling — new TAO keeps flowing in.

Bearish effect: It's funded by inflation, not real demand

That 247 TAO/day isn't coming from customers paying for AI services. It's newly minted TAO. So the "cash injection" is really just inflation on the TAO side. If TAO's price drops (because TAO itself is being diluted), the dollar value of that daily injection drops too. It's a circular system — tokens backing tokens backing tokens, with no external revenue anchor.

The tiebreaker: Staking flows

Since Nov 2025, Bittensor uses "TaoFlow" — emission share is based on net staking inflows, not just price. If people stake more TAO into Templar, it gets a bigger share of daily emissions. If they unstake, the share drops. This is why the 6.86% number isn't fixed — it's a market signal of confidence. Right now, Templar has the highest share, meaning the market is voting it the most valuable subnet.

Bottom line: TAO inflows support the alpha price mechanically (more reserves in the pool), but the support is only as strong as TAO's own value, which itself depends on the overall network delivering real utility. It's reflexive — works great on the way up, unwinds on the way down.

Covenant-72B: What They Built and What It Cost

The Achievement in Plain English

On March 10, 2026, Templar finished training a 72-billion-parameter AI model called Covenant-72B. What makes it special isn't the model itself — it's how it was trained:

  • No data center. Instead of renting a massive GPU cluster from Google or AWS, they used ~20 independent people around the world, each contributing 8 high-end GPUs (NVIDIA B200s). ~70 unique participants joined over the course of the run.
  • No whitelist. Anyone with the right hardware could join or leave at any time. Previous decentralized training runs required an invite. This was fully open.
  • Commodity internet. Peers communicated over regular internet connections using a clever compression algorithm (SparseLoCo) that shrinks data by 146x, so bandwidth wasn't a bottleneck.

Jensen Huang (NVIDIA CEO), Chamath Palihapitiya, and Jack Clark (Anthropic co-founder) all publicly acknowledged the milestone.

How Good Is the Model?

Covenant-72B scores 67.1 on MMLU, a standard AI benchmark. To put that in perspective:

Translation: Covenant-72B is roughly as smart as Meta's LLaMA-2-70B from July 2023 — about 3 years behind today's frontier models (which score 90+). It actually beats LLaMA-2 on math (26.3 vs 10.7) despite training on half the data.

The model isn't commercially competitive with what's available today. But that's not really the point — the point is proving that permissionless, decentralized training works at meaningful scale. Whether it can close the gap to frontier models is the open question.

What Did It Cost?

There are two ways to think about the cost:

If You Rented GPUs Normally
~$1.3M

~300K B200 GPU-hours at $3-5/hr cloud rates. For context, Meta spent ~$2M training LLaMA-2-70B on A100s in 2023 — but with 2x more training data.

vs
What Bittensor Actually Paid
~$6.5M

~22,500 TAO in emissions over ~91 days at current prices. This TAO was newly minted — not from a treasury. The cost was borne by all TAO/alpha holders through inflation.

That's a 3-5x "decentralization premium" over just renting GPUs from a cloud provider. You're paying extra for:

  • Permissionless participation (no one can shut it down)
  • Blockchain coordination overhead (Gauntlet scoring, on-chain verification)
  • Incentive overpayment (need to attract enough GPUs to the network)
  • Communication overhead (~5.5% of time lost to syncing gradients over internet)

The Scaling Problem

Covenant-72B needed each participant to have 8 NVIDIA B200 GPUs wired together with high-speed NVLink inside a single machine. That's a ~$200-300K setup. The clever SparseLoCo algorithm handles communication between machines over the internet, but within each machine, you need all 8 GPUs tightly connected to fit the model in memory.

Here's the problem: bigger models need bigger machines.

The paradox: The whole pitch of Templar is "anyone can help train AI." But as models get bigger, fewer and fewer people have big enough machines to participate. At frontier scale (700B+), you're back to needing the same massive clusters that Google and Meta use — defeating the purpose. Whether the team can find creative solutions to this (model parallelism tricks, heterogeneous hardware support) will determine if this scales beyond a proof of concept.

So Is This a Good Investment?

Bull Case

  • Genuine technical milestone — largest permissionless training ever
  • Endorsed by Jensen Huang, Chamath, Jack Clark
  • Highest emission share of any Bittensor subnet (market's vote of confidence)
  • Halving already cut emissions 50%; supply growth is decelerating
  • Only 20% of supply out = you're early if the thesis plays out
  • Templar Crusades (upcoming competition format) could attract more developers

Bear Case

  • Model quality ~3 years behind frontier — not commercially useful today
  • ~80% supply dilution ahead (FDV is 5x current market cap)
  • Training cost 3-5x more than centralized — hard to justify economically
  • Scaling to bigger models undermines the decentralization thesis
  • No revenue — everything is funded by token inflation
  • Extreme Greed sentiment (81.2) after a 194% weekly pump
